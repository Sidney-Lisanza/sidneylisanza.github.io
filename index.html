About

Bridging computational design and experimental reality.

I’m a computational biochemist and machine learning scientist with a PhD in Biochemistry from the University of Washington, where I trained in the Baker Lab at the Institute for Protein Design.

My work sits at the interface of generative modeling, protein structure, and therapeutic discovery. I like problems where model development has to stay tightly connected to real experimental constraints.

At Prescient Design, I work with collaborators across biology, chemistry, and platform teams to translate target-product goals into model requirements and iterative design strategies.

Earlier, I helped develop deep learning methods for functional site scaffolding and sequence-structure diffusion, including work that led to ProteinGenerator and related publications in Science, Nature Biotechnology, and beyond.

Work

A research profile shaped by models, collaboration, and iteration.

Now

Prescient Design, Genentech

Developing machine learning methods for protein design and coordinating design-build-test workflows with experimental collaborators. The through-line is practical translation: turning biological constraints into model objectives that can survive iterative rounds of testing.

PhD

Institute for Protein Design, University of Washington

Built generative modeling methods for de novo protein design, especially approaches that jointly model sequence and structure. This work included RoseTTAFold sequence-space diffusion and functional site scaffolding with deep learning.

Earlier

Research and teaching across computation and biochemistry

Worked on protein crystal design, signaling dynamics with FRET-based biosensors, and teaching in both biochemistry and protein design settings. I enjoy the part of science where methods become shared tools for other people.

Selected Focus

What I’m especially excited by.

Constraint-aware design

Building systems that start from real target requirements, not toy objectives, and use those constraints to shape model design and experimental follow-up.

Protein generation

Exploring how sequence, structure, and function can be modeled jointly to make protein generation more controllable and more useful for discovery.

Cross-functional science

Working in the handoff zones between machine learning, computation, chemistry, and experimental biology where alignment matters as much as raw model performance.
